Starry Night

Artist: Vincent van Gogh

Technique: Oil Painting

Style: Post-Impressionism

Description:

Vincent van Gogh’s The Starry Night is one of the most iconic and emotionally powerful paintings in art history. Created in June 1889 during Van Gogh's stay at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, France, it captures a swirling night sky filled with energy, emotion, and movement, seen from the window of his room.

Historical Context:

Van Gogh painted The Starry Night while voluntarily confined in an asylum after a series of mental breakdowns, including the famous incident in which he cut off part of his own ear. Despite the dark circumstances, the painting radiates awe and wonder. Interestingly, Van Gogh did not consider The Starry Night one of his best works. He felt it was overly stylized and even called it a failure in his letters. However, it later became one of his most celebrated masterpieces and a defining image of modern art.

Mona Lisa

Artist: Leonardo da Vinci

Technique: Oil Painting

Style: Renaissance

Description:

Leonardo da Vinci’s Mona Lisa is arguably the most famous and recognized painting in the world. Created between 1503 and 1506 (possibly continuing until 1517), this iconic portrait has fascinated viewers for centuries due to its subject’s mysterious expression, innovative technique, and subtle detail.

Historical Context

Leonardo likely carried the painting with him for years, including during his final stay in France under the patronage of King Francis I. After Leonardo's death in 1519, the painting entered the French royal collection and eventually found its way to the Louvre Museum in Paris, where it remains today. In 1911, the painting was famously stolen by an Italian handyman who believed it should be returned to Italy. It was recovered two years later and became even more famous because of the incident. Since then, it's survived attacks, protests, and the test of time.

The Persistence of Memory

Artist: Salvador Dalí

Technique: Oil Painting

Style: Surrealism

Description:

Salvador Dalí’s The Persistence of Memory (1931) is one of the most iconic and recognizable works of Surrealist art. Often referred to as the “melting clocks painting,” it presents a dreamlike world where time appears to have lost all meaning—challenging the viewer’s perception of reality, time, and memory.

Historical Context

Dalí painted The Persistence of Memory at the age of 27, during a particularly prolific period. He was deeply influenced by the theories of Sigmund Freud and the workings of the unconscious mind. Dalí’s dreamlike world, filled with symbolic objects, became his signature style. He described his works as “hand-painted dream photographs,” aiming to create realistic images of fantastical, irrational ideas.

The Girl with a Pearl Earring

Artist:Johannes Vermeer

Technique:Realism

Style: Baroque / Dutch Golden Age

Description:

Johannes Vermeer’s Girl with a Pearl Earring (circa 1665) is one of the most beloved and mysterious portraits in Western art. Often called the “Mona Lisa of the North,” this painting captivates viewers with its simplicity, intimacy, and the enigmatic expression of the girl.

Historical Context

Little is known about the identity of the girl. Some theories suggest she may have been Vermeer’s daughter or a maid, but most scholars agree she was likely a model created for artistic study rather than a portrait of a known individual. Johannes Vermeer, though not widely known during his lifetime, is now celebrated for his ability to capture quiet, contemplative moments. He created fewer than 40 paintings in total, which adds to the rarity and value of his work.

The Scream

Artist:Edvard Munch

Technique:Tempera and pastel

Style:Expressionism

Description:

Edvard Munch’s The Scream (first version created in 1893) is one of the most powerful visual expressions of human anxiety, despair, and existential fear ever put to canvas. It has become an icon of modern art—instantly recognizable and endlessly interpreted.

Versions & Medium:

There are main versions of The Scream created between 1893 and 1910:

  1. 1893 (tempera on cardboard)– The most famous version, located in the National Gallery, Oslo.
  2. 1893 (pastel on cardboard) – Owned by the Munch Museum, Oslo.
  3. 1895 (pastel)– Privately owned (sold at auction for nearly $120 million in 2012).
  4. 1910 (painted version) – Also held at the Munch Museum.

    5. Munch also created lithographs, allowing the image to be reproduced and distributed more widely.

Creation of Adam

Artist: Michaelangelo

Technique: Fresco

Style: High Renaissance

Description:

Michelangelo’s The Creation of Adam (circa 1511–1512) is one of the most famous and celebrated frescoes in the history of art. Located on the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel in Vatican City, this iconic scene has become a symbol of humanity, divine spark, and the relationship between man and God.

Historical Context:

Michelangelo painted the Creation of Adam as part of the larger Sistine Chapel ceiling, commissioned by Pope Julius II. The ceiling contains more than 300 figures and nine central scenes from the Book of Genesis, including The Creation of the Sun and Moon, The Fall of Man, and The Great Flood. Although initially reluctant to take the commission (Michelangelo considered himself a sculptor), he completed the monumental ceiling between 1508 and 1512, working mostly alone while standing or lying on scaffolding.

The Last Supper

Artist: Leonardo Da Vinci

Technique: Wall Painting

Style: High Renaissance

Description:

Leonardo da Vinci’s The Last Supper (painted between 1495 and 1498) is one of the most revered and influential masterpieces of the Renaissance. This iconic mural captures the dramatic and emotional moment Jesus announces that one of the twelve apostles will betray him. It's not just a religious image—it’s a groundbreaking study of human emotion, storytelling through art, and mastery of perspective.

Historical Context:

Leonardo painted The Last Supper during a time of flourishing art, science, and religious influence in Renaissance Italy. Commissioned by Duke Ludovico Sforza, it decorated the wall of the monastery’s refectory (dining hall), where monks would eat while contemplating this sacred scene. It’s a visual meditation on betrayal, sacrifice, and faith, capturing the pivotal moment in the New Testament (John 13:21).

Spoliarium

Artist: Juan Luna

Technique: Oil Painting

Style: Romanticism / Realism

Description:

Juan Luna’s Spoliarium (1884) is one of the most iconic and historically significant paintings in Philippine art. Measuring a massive 4.22 meters tall and 7.675 meters wide, this epic oil on canvas is a powerful allegory of colonial oppression, human suffering, and the struggle for freedom and dignity.

Historical Context:

Juan Luna painted the Spoliarium while he was studying in Madrid. It was his entry to the prestigious Exposición Nacional de Bellas Artes in 1884, where it won the first gold medal—a monumental achievement for a Filipino under Spanish rule. The painting became a symbol of Filipino pride and awakening, especially among the members of the Propaganda Movement, which sought reform and recognition for Filipinos. José Rizal, Luna’s close friend and the Philippines’ national hero, described the Spoliarium as: “the essence of our social, moral, and political life: humanity unredeemed, reason and idealism trampled by force, and the blood of the innocent flowing.” It stirred a generation of intellectuals and revolutionaries, fueling a growing sense of Filipino identity and resistance.

American Gothic

Artist: Grant Wood

Technique: Oil Paintig

Style: Baroque

Description:

Grant Wood’s American Gothic (1930) is one of the most recognizable and parodied paintings in American art. With its hauntingly still atmosphere and deeply symbolic imagery, it captures a slice of rural American life during the Great Depression—and invites viewers to question what's beneath its solemn surface.

Inpiration & Creation:

Grant Wood, an Iowa-born artist, was inspired when he saw a small white cottage in Eldon, Iowa, with a Gothic-style window. He imagined who might live there and asked his sister Nan Wood Graham and his dentist Dr. Byron McKeeby to pose as a father and daughter (not husband and wife, as commonly assumed). He painted them in traditional, even outdated, 19th-century clothing to evoke old-fashioned values and a timeless rural spirit.

The Great Wave off Kanagawa

Artist: Hokusai

Technique: OIl Painting

Style:Ukiyo-e (Japanese Art)

Description:

Katsushika Hokusai’s The Great Wave off Kanagawa (c. 1831) is one of the most famous and celebrated works of Japanese art—and arguably one of the most recognized images in the world. This iconic woodblock print captures the raw power of nature, the smallness of humanity, and the beauty of traditional Japanese art and craftsmanship.

Historical Context:

  • Created during Japan’s Edo period, when the country was isolated from the rest of the world under the Tokugawa shogunate.
  • This era saw a rise in urban culture, and ukiyo-e prints like this were affordable art for common people—not elite commissions.
  • The print reflects both spiritual themes (like reverence for nature and Mount Fuji) and everyday life (like fishermen at work).

The Kiss

Artist: Gustav Klimt

Technique: Oil and Gold Leaf Painting

Style: Symbolism / Art Nouveau

Description:

Gustav Klimt’s The Kiss (1907–1908) is a dazzling, golden masterpiece that captures a moment of deep intimacy, love, and spiritual connection. Considered the pinnacle of Klimt’s Golden Phase, it is one of the most iconic and beloved paintings in art history—a symbol of passion, sensuality, and unity.

Artist & Inpiration:

Gustav Klimt (1862–1918) was an Austrian symbolist painter known for his sensual, decorative, and often controversial works. The Kiss was painted at the height of his career. It’s widely believed the model for the woman was Emilie Flöge, Klimt’s lifelong companion and muse—though this has never been definitively confirmed.

Arrangement in Grey and Black No 1

Artist: James McNeill Whistler

Technique: Oil Painting

Style: Tonalism / Aestheticism

Description:

James McNeill Whistler’s Arrangement in Grey and Black No. 1 (1871), more famously known as "Whistler’s Mother," is one of the most iconic portraits in American and European art. Despite its seemingly quiet and restrained subject—a seated woman in profile—it’s a powerful study of composition, mood, and maternal dignity.

Historical Context:

  • Painted in London in 1871
  • Whistler intended it to be a study in design, but his decision to use his mother as the model gave the work a deeper, emotional resonance.
  • The painting was initially rejected by the Royal Academy but later became one of the most recognized artworks in the world.

Wanderer above the Sea of Fog

Artist: Caspar David Friedrich

Technique: Oil Painting

Style: Romanticism

Description:

Caspar David Friedrich’s Wanderer above the Sea of Fog (1818) is a hauntingly beautiful and iconic painting that captures the essence of Romanticism—a movement that emphasized emotion, nature, and the sublime. The painting is both a self-reflective journey and a powerful symbol of human existence, standing on the edge of the unknown.

Historical Context:

  • Painted in post-Napoleonic Europe, when Romanticism emerged as a reaction to the Enlightenment’s focus on reason.
  • Friedrich, a devout Christian and deeply philosophical painter, saw nature as a pathway to the divine.
  • The work became a visual manifesto for Romantic ideals: emotion, introspection, solitude, and reverence for nature.

The Birth of Venus

Artist: Sandro Botticelli

Technique: Tempera on canvas

Style: Early Renaissance

Description:

"The Birth of Venus" is a masterpiece by Sandro Botticelli, depicting the goddess Venus emerging from the sea upon a shell, symbolizing her birth. The painting is renowned for its graceful composition and delicate use of color, embodying the ideals of beauty and harmony characteristic of the Early Renaissance.

Historical Context:

Created around 1485, the painting reflects the humanist interests of the Medici family, who were prominent patrons of the arts in Florence. It draws inspiration from classical mythology and literature, showcasing the Renaissance fascination with antiquity and the revival of Greco-Roman ideals.

Guernica

Artist: Pablo Picasso

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Cubism / Surrealism

Description:

"Guernica" is a powerful anti-war painting by Pablo Picasso, portraying the horrors of the bombing of the Basque town of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War. The monochromatic palette and distorted figures convey the chaos and suffering inflicted upon innocent civilians.

Historical Context:

Painted in 1937, "Guernica" was Picasso's response to the aerial bombing of the town by Nazi German and Italian Fascist forces. Commissioned for the Spanish Pavilion at the 1937 Paris World's Fair, the painting became an emblem of the tragedies of war and a symbol of peace.

Liberty Leading the People

Artist: Eugène Delacroix

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Romanticism

Description:

This iconic painting by Delacroix depicts a personified Liberty leading a diverse group of people over a barricade, symbolizing unity and the fight for freedom. The dynamic composition and vivid colors exemplify the Romantic style's emphasis on emotion and movement.

Historical Context:

Painted in 1830, the artwork commemorates the July Revolution in France, which led to the overthrow of King Charles X. It serves as a political statement and a celebration of the people's uprising, embodying the spirit of revolution and the pursuit of liberty.

Las Meninas

Artist: Diego Velázquez

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Baroque

Description:

"Las Meninas" is a complex and enigmatic painting that portrays Infanta Margarita Teresa surrounded by her entourage, with Velázquez himself depicted at his easel. The composition plays with perspective and viewer engagement, making it a subject of extensive art historical analysis.

Historical Context:

Completed in 1656, the painting offers insight into the Spanish court's inner workings during King Philip IV's reign. Its innovative approach to composition and perspective has influenced countless artists and is considered a pinnacle of Baroque art.

The Night Watch

Artist: Rembrandt van Rijn

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Baroque

Description:

“The Night Watch” is one of Rembrandt's most famous and ambitious works, notable for its grand scale, masterful use of light and shadow, and the dynamic composition of a militia company preparing for action. Rather than a static group portrait, Rembrandt infuses the scene with movement and personality, capturing both the collective and individual characters of the company.

Historical Context:

Painted in 1642, during the Dutch Golden Age, the work reflects the civic pride of Amsterdam's elite militia groups. It broke conventions of group portraiture at the time, stirring controversy and admiration alike. Over the centuries, the painting has endured damage and restoration but remains an iconic treasure of the Rijksmuseum and a symbol of the artistic innovation of the 17th-century Netherlands.

Nighthawks

Artist: Edward Hopper

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Realism

Description:

“Nighthawks” depicts a quiet, late-night diner scene with four figures, each isolated in their own thoughts despite their proximity. Hopper’s meticulous attention to light, shadow, and architecture emphasizes urban loneliness and detachment, evoking a haunting mood of stillness and introspection that has become iconic in American art.

Historical Context:

Painted in 1942 shortly after the attack on Pearl Harbor, “Nighthawks” captured a mood of anxiety and solitude that resonated with the American public. Hopper denied any specific narrative but acknowledged the painting’s deep psychological tension. Today, it's a centerpiece of the Art Institute of Chicago’s collection and a powerful symbol of 20th-century urban experience.

The Raft of the Medusa

Artist: Théodore Géricault

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Romanticism

Description:

This emotionally intense painting portrays survivors of a French naval disaster stranded at sea. Géricault masterfully captures a moment of desperation and faint hope, with figures dramatically arranged in a pyramid-like structure, their bodies illuminated by dramatic lighting. The raw realism and expressive detail make the composition hauntingly powerful.

Historical Context:

Painted in 1818–1819, the artwork was based on the real-life 1816 wreck of the frigate Méduse. It became a symbol of political critique, exposing government incompetence and igniting public outrage. Géricault conducted extensive research, including interviews with survivors and morgue visits, to achieve its chilling authenticity.

Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette

Artist: Pierre-Auguste Renoir

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Impressionism

Description:

This lively and colorful painting captures Parisians enjoying a Sunday afternoon at a popular dance garden in Montmartre. Renoir uses dappled light and quick brushstrokes to convey motion, joy, and the festive atmosphere of urban leisure. It’s a celebration of life, youth, and community, rendered with warmth and spontaneity.

Historical Context:

Completed in 1876, this work epitomizes Impressionist ideals—depicting modern life and fleeting moments. Renoir painted many of his friends as models, embedding his social circle into a scene of working-class leisure. Now housed in the Musée d'Orsay, it remains one of the most beloved images of 19th-century Parisian culture.

Landscape of the Heart

Artist: BenCab (Benedicto Cabrera)

Technique: Acrylic on canvas

Style: Contemporary Figurative Expressionism

Description:

“Landscape of the Heart” is a poignant exploration of emotion and identity. In this work, BenCab employs expressive brushwork and layered textures to depict a solitary figure enveloped in flowing fabrics, set against a backdrop that merges the physical and emotional landscapes. The composition evokes a sense of introspection and longing, characteristic of BenCab's exploration of the human condition.

Historical Context:

As a National Artist for Visual Arts in the Philippines, BenCab has significantly influenced contemporary Filipino art. His works often reflect social realities and personal narratives, bridging traditional themes with modern expression. “Landscape of the Heart” exemplifies his commitment to portraying the nuanced experiences of individuals, particularly within the context of Filipino culture and history.

The Red Fuji

Artist: Katsushika Hokusai

Technique: Color woodblock print (nishiki-e)

Style: Ukiyo-e

Description:

“The Red Fuji,” also known as “South Wind, Clear Sky” (Gaifū kaisei), is one of Hokusai’s most iconic prints from his celebrated series *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji*. The artwork captures Mount Fuji bathed in the warm glow of early morning sunlight during late summer or early autumn, turning its slopes a vivid red. The composition is minimalist yet powerful, with the majestic mountain dominating the frame, set against a clear blue sky adorned with stylized white clouds. The simplicity and bold use of color exemplify Hokusai's mastery in conveying natural beauty through woodblock printing.

Historical Context:

Created around 1830–1831 during Japan's Edo period, “The Red Fuji” reflects the cultural and spiritual significance of Mount Fuji in Japanese society. Hokusai's *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji* series was groundbreaking in its focus on landscape as a primary subject, a departure from the traditional emphasis on figures in ukiyo-e art. This particular print, along with “The Great Wave off Kanagawa,” played a pivotal role in influencing Western artists and contributed to the Japonisme movement in Europe. The series showcases Hokusai's innovative approach to perspective and his deep reverence for nature, solidifying his legacy as one of Japan's greatest artists.

The Burial of the Sardine

Artist: Francisco de Goya

Technique: Oil on wood panel

Style: Romanticism

Description:

“The Burial of the Sardine” depicts the final event of Madrid’s Carnival, a satirical procession marking the end of indulgence before Lent. The painting shows a lively crowd of masked revelers dancing and parading a large sardine effigy through the streets. Goya captures the chaotic energy and masked anonymity of the participants, blending festive exuberance with a sense of underlying tension.

Historical Context:

Created between 1812 and 1819, this work reflects the Spanish tradition of Carnival, which often involved subversive and satirical elements. The painting's vibrant colors and dynamic composition contrast with Goya's darker works, such as the "Black Paintings," showcasing his versatility and engagement with both lighthearted and critical themes. The piece is now housed in the Real Academia de Bellas Artes de San Fernando in Madrid.

Composition VII

Artist: Wassily Kandinsky

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Abstract Expressionism

Description:

“Composition VII” is a monumental work by Wassily Kandinsky, created in 1913. Measuring 200 × 300 cm, it is considered one of the pinnacles of abstract art. The painting is a dynamic interplay of swirling forms and vibrant colors, embodying Kandinsky's belief that art could express the spiritual and emotional realms. The composition is filled with energy, with no clear focal point, allowing the viewer's eye to move freely across the canvas, experiencing the tumultuous yet harmonious arrangement of shapes and hues.

Historical Context:

Completed just before World War I, “Composition VII” reflects Kandinsky's deep engagement with spiritual and philosophical ideas. He had been exploring abstraction as a means to convey inner emotions and universal truths, moving away from representational art. The painting is part of his "Composition" series, which he considered the culmination of his artistic journey. Kandinsky's work had a profound influence on the development of abstract art, paving the way for future generations of artists to explore non-representational forms.

Mother and Child

Artist: Fernando Amorsolo

Technique: Oil on canvas

Style: Romanticism

Description:

“Mother and Child” is a poignant portrayal of maternal love and care. The painting depicts a mother cradling her child in a serene rural setting, capturing the tenderness and devotion inherent in the mother-child bond. Amorsolo's use of soft lighting and warm tones enhances the emotional depth of the scene, highlighting the nurturing relationship between the two figures.

Historical Context:

Created in the early 20th century, this artwork reflects Amorsolo's commitment to depicting Filipino life and values. As the first National Artist for Painting in the Philippines, Amorsolo's works often celebrated the beauty of rural life and the strength of familial bonds. “Mother and Child” stands as a testament to his ability to convey profound human emotions through art, resonating with audiences both locally and internationally.

The Tree of Life

Artist: Gustav Klimt

Technique: Oil on canvas with gold leaf

Style: Symbolism / Art Nouveau

Description:

“The Tree of Life” is a striking example of Gustav Klimt’s symbolic and decorative style. The painting features a stylized tree with swirling branches, symbolizing the interconnectedness of life, death, and rebirth. The intricate patterns and use of gold leaf create a sense of opulence and transcendence, inviting viewers to contemplate the cyclical nature of existence.

Historical Context:

Completed in 1909, this work was part of a larger commission for the Stoclet Palace in Brussels, designed by architect Josef Hoffmann. Klimt’s contribution included a series of mosaics for the dining room, with “The Tree of Life” serving as a central motif. The piece reflects the Art Nouveau movement’s emphasis on organic forms and the integration of art into everyday life, embodying the concept of Gesamtkunstwerk, or total work of art.
